d-Harish-Chandra series describe unipotent l-blocks of a finite reductive group $­Юљє(­Юћй_q)$ for $l|╬д_d(q)$ (at least, when l is not too small which means mostly not a bad prime for ­Юљє). Some of the facts stated below are still partly conjectural, we do not try to distinguish precisely what has been established and what is still conjectural.

If (­ЮљІ,╬╗) is a d-cuspidal pair then the constituents of the Lusztig induced $R_­ЮљІ^­Юљє(╬╗)$ are called a d-Harish-Chandra series; they form the unipotent part of an l-block of $­Юљє^F$ (Brou├Е et al., 1993; 5.24). It is conjectured (Brou├Е and Michel, 1997; 5.7) (and proven in some cases) that the $­Юљє^F$-endomorphism algebra of the l-adic cohomology of the variety ­ЮљЌ which defines the Lusztig induction is a d-cyclotomic Hecke algebra $H_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗)$ for the group $W_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗):=N_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗)/­ЮљІ$, which is a complex reflection group РђЊ- here d-cyclotomic means that the parameters of $H_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗)$ are monomials in q and that $H_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗)$ specializes to the algebra of $W_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗)$ for $qРєд╬Х_d$.

It follows that the decomposition of the Lusztig induction is of the form $R_­ЮљІ^­Юљє(╬╗)=РѕЉ_{¤ЋРѕѕIrr(W_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗))}(-1)^{nрхЕ} ¤Ћ(1)╬│рхЕ,$ where ╬│рхЕ is a unipotent character of ­Юљє^F attached to ¤Ћ and where nрхЕ is the degree $H^{nрхЕ}_c(­ЮљЌ)$ where ╬│рхЕ occurss; and further for any ¤Ћ we have $R_­ЮљІ^­Юљє(╬╗)(1)= (-1)^{nрхЕ} ╬│рхЕ(1)SрхЕ$ where SрхЕ is the Schur element of the character of $H_­Юљє(­ЮљІ,╬╗)$ which deforms to ¤Ћ. Chevie.Uch.cuspidal_data Рђћ Function

cuspidal_data(W[,d[,ad]];proper=false,all=false)

returns named tuples (levi=LF,cuspidal=╬╗,d=d) where LF is a d-split Levi (with d-center of dimension ad if ad is given) and ╬╗ is a d-cuspidal character of LF. If d=1 this returns ordinary cuspidal characters. The character ╬╗ is given as its index in the list of unipotent characters. If d was given as an integer, it is returned as a Root1 representing E(d).

If the keyword proper=true is given, only the data where LF!=W (or equivalently ad>0) are returned.

If d is omitted, data for all d orders of eigenvalues of elements of W is returned. If in addition the keyword argument all=true is given, data for all eigenvalues of elements of W is returned.

Chevie.Symbols.ennola Рђћ Function

ennola(S::CharSymbol)

Ennola of e-symbol S (of content 1 or 0) The order of Ennola (order of center of reflection group) is computed automatically: it is e for content 1 and gcd(e,rank(S)) for content 0.

source

ennola(W[,z=E(gcd(degrees(W)))])

Let W be an irreducible spetsial reflection group or coset, and z the generator of the center of W, viewed as the root of unity E(gcd(degrees(W)))]). Let ­ЮћЙ be the spets attached to W. A property checked case-by case is that, for a unipotent character ╬│ of ­ЮћЙ with polynomial generic degree deg ╬│(q) , deg ╬│(zq) is equal to ┬▒deg ╬│'(q) for another unipotent character ╬│'; ┬▒╬│' is called the Ennola transform of ╬│. For W a Weyl group, the spets ­ЮћЙ is a finite reductive group, in which case z=-1 if -1 is in W and z=1 otherwise. The function returns the signed permutation e done by ennola on the unipotent degrees (as an SPerm of 1:length(UnipotentCharacters(W))).

The SPerm e is not uniquely determined by the degrees since two degrees may be equal, but is uniquely determined by some additional axioms that we do not recall here (they include a description of the Ennola-permutation in terms of the Z-based rings attached to each Lusztig family).

If a second argument z is given, it should be a power of the default z and the corresponding power of e is returned.

julia> ennola(rootdatum("3D4"))
SPerm{Int64}: (3,-4)(5,-5)(6,-6)(7,-8)

julia> ennola(complex_reflection_group(14))
SPerm{Int64}: (2,43,-14,16,41,34)(3,35,40,18,-11,42)(4,-37,25,-17,-26,-36)(5,-6,-79)(7,-7)(8,-74)(9,-73)(10,-52,13,31,-50,29)(12,53,15,32,-51,-30)(19,71,70,21,67,68,20,69,72)(22,-39,27,-33,-28,-38)(23,24,-66,-23,-24,66)(44,46,49,-44,-46,-49)(45,48,47,-45,-48,-47)(54,-63,-55,-57,62,-56)(58,-65,-59,-61,64,-60)(75,-77)(76,-76)(78,-78)

The above example shows that it may happen that the order of z-Ennola (here 18) is greater than the order of z (here 6); this is related to the presence of irrationalities qРЁЊ in the character table of the spetsial Hecke algebra of W.

For a non-irreducible group, z-ennola is defined if z can be considered an element of the the centre of each irreducible component.
